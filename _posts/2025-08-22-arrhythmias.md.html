Canine & Feline Cardiology: Arrhythmias

Diagnoses Pathogenesis Diagnosis Treatment Prognosis
(General) Arrhythmias Disruptions in normal conduction; Sinus node → AV node → His bundle → Right & left bundle branches → Purkinje fibres.
Due to structural heart disease (cardiomyopathy, endocardiosis), electrolyte imbalances (high K⁺, low Ca²⁺), systemic disease (hypothyroidism, sepsis), or autonomic tone changes (vagal stimulation).		 ECG; differentiates sinus vs. premature beats vs. bradyarrhythmias vs. tachyarrhythmias.
Holter monitoring; 24-hr ECG (intermittent arrhythmias)
Echocardiography; assess structural heart disease.		 Bradyarrhythmias: Atropine (vagolytic therapy), pacemaker if severe.
Main pacemaker indications: 3rd-degree AV block, 2nd-degree AV block type II, sick sinus syndrome, atrial standstill.
Tachyarrhythmias: Beta-blockers (atenolol), calcium channel blockers (diltiazem), antiarrhythmics (lidocaine, procainamide, amiodarone).		 Variable. Some benign (respiratory sinus arrhythmia), others indicate severe underlying disease (3rd-degree AV block, ventricular fibrillation).
Tachyarrhythmias Supraventricular (Atrial fibrillation/flutter, tachycardia) or ventricular (tachycardia, Ventricular premature complexes, Ventricular fibrillation, Torsades de Pointes).
Causes: Atrial enlargement (large-breed cardiomyopathy), systemic disease, electrolyte imbalances, structural heart disease (DCM, ARVC).
AFib is most common supraventricular tachyarrhythmia. Primary idiopathic Afib often asymptomatic in large breed dogs.
Torsades de Pointes = rare polymorphic VTach associated with prolonged QT (often from hypokalemia/magnesemia, medications, or systemic causes)		 ECG:
AFib: No P waves, irregularly irregular R-R intervals, narrow QRS. (Auscultation sounds chaotic - like shoes in a dryer.)
VTach: Wide QRS, dissociated P waves. Sustained VTach; ≥3 VPCs for >30 seconds.
Torsades: prolonged QT interval + cyclic variation of QRS ('twisting' of QRS around isoelectric line).		 Supraventricular tachyarrhythmias:  Beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin (incr vagal tone), amiodarone (antiarrhythmic).
Ventricular tachycardia and Ventricular premature complexes: Lidocaine (antiarrhythmic), procainamide (antiarrhythmic), mexiletine (oral lidocaine analogue), beta-blockers.
VFib/Torsades: Electrical cardioversion, IV magnesium sulphate.		 AFib: Manageable but usually secondary to heart disease.
VFib: Fatal if untreated.
Torsades: Treatable if underlying cause (e.g. hypokalaemia) is corrected
Bradyarrhythmias Increased parasympathetic tone, AV nodal disease, sinus node dysfunction (age-related degeneration/fibrosis).
Causes include hypoxemia, hyperkalaemia, hypothyroidism, GDV, neoplasia.
Pacemaker predis: Mini Schnauzer, Cockers, Westie, Pug.
Atrial standstill can be caused by hyperkalaemia from urinary obstruction or hypoadrenocorticism.		 ECG:
1st degree AV block; increased PQ interval (cannot auscultate)
2nd degree Mobitz type 1; increasing PQ interval until 1 P wave with no QRS
2nd degree Mobitz type 2; randomly unconducted P wave
3rd degree (complete block); P waves completely unassociated with QRS (P waves can be hidden within QRS).
Hyperkalaemia-related atrial standstill shows no P waves.		 Vagally-mediated blocks; Atropine.
3rd-degree AV block, persistent 2nd-degree AV block type II (or sick sinus syndrome); Pacemaker.
3rd degree AV block medical therapy; increase HR with atropine or isoproterenol (limited success).		 Good if vagally mediated (resolves with atropine).
Pacemaker improves prognosis for bradyarrhythmias.
Poor without pacemaker for 3rd-degree AV block.
Sick Sinus Syndrome (SSS) Dysfunction of the sinus node causing inappropriate bradycardia, often alternating with paroxysmal tachycardia (bradycardia-tachycardia syndrome)
Predis; Mini Schnauzer, Cocker, Westie, Pug.		 ECG showing a mix of sinus arrest, intermittent AV blocks, and escape beats (rescue heart from asystole).
Hx; Syncope		 Definitive; Pacemaker for bradycardia.
Antiarrhythmics for tachycardic episodes after pacemaker implanted.		 Good with pacemaker.
Premature Beats Ectopic pacemaker activity outside the sinus node.
Supraventricular or ventricular origin.
VPCs seen in non-cardiac conditions; GDV, neoplasia, anaemia, pancreatitis, hypoxia, pain.		 Supraventricular premature complexes (SVPCs): Tall narrow QRS, abnormal P wave.
Ventricular premature complexes (VPCs): Wide, bizarre QRS, no associated P wave. Can also be in couplets, triplets, quadruplets, run or ventricular tachycardia (6+).		 SVPCs: Usually benign, treat underlying cause if needed.
VPCs: Lidocaine or beta-blockers (sotalol) if frequent.		 SVPCs: Often incidental.
VPCs: Concerning if frequent or associated with structural disease.
Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy (ARVC) (Fibro)fatty infiltration of the right ventricle leads to electrical instability.
Predis; Boxers (heritable; autosomal dominant).		 Syncope, possible systolic dysfunction. Can be asymptomatic before sudden cardiac events.
ECG; Right ventricular arrhythmias.
Holter monitor for intermittent arrhythmias.
No definitive cure.
Antiarrhythmics: Sotalol, mexiletine (often combined with atenolol).		 High risk of sudden death due to fatal arrhythmias.
Other ECG Abnormalities Electrical disturbances from structural, metabolic, or autonomic changes. Small QRS complexes: Pericardial effusion, pleural effusion, fat, hypothyroidism.
Myocardial hypoxia/ischemia; ST elevation or depression.
Wide QRS with a P wave; Bundle branch block.
