Patent Ductus Arteriosus in Dogs

The tiny fetal vessel that never got the memo to close.

You’ve probably heard the term “PDA” thrown around in cardiology lectures — and no, we’re not talking about public displays of affection (although these dogs do need a little extra love).

Patent Ductus Arteriosus is a persistent fetal blood vessel that can lead to some big-time problems if it doesn’t shut down after birth.

🧠 What is the ductus arteriosus?

In the womb, a puppy doesn’t breathe air, so the blood doesn’t need to go to the lungs. The ductus arteriosus is a fetal shortcut that connects the pulmonary artery to the aorta, bypassing the lungs. Normally, it closes soon after birth. When it doesn’t? That’s PDA.

🔄 What goes wrong?

You get a left-to-right shunt — oxygenated blood from the aorta re-enters the pulmonary circulation. This causes volume overload, left-sided heart enlargement, pulmonary oedema, and heart failure if untreated.

🐶 Who gets it?

PDA is congenital. It’s more common in females and certain breeds (e.g., Poodles, Maltese, GSDs, Pomeranians).

👂 What will you hear?

A continuous machinery murmur over the left heart base — often loud, and classic for PDA.

🖼️ Diagnostics

  • Echo: confirms shunting + chamber enlargement
  • Rads: left-sided heart enlargement, big vessels, pulmonary oedema
  • ECG: tall R waves (LV enlargement), arrhythmias in advanced cases

🛠️ Treatment

Preferred method is transcatheter device closure (e.g., Amplatz). Surgery is an option too. Early treatment = excellent prognosis. Don’t wait — untreated PDA can become irreversible.

TL;DR

  • Congenital vascular anomaly
  • Left-to-right shunt → left heart overload → CHF
  • Continuous murmur, left heart enlargement
  • Fixable with early intervention!
